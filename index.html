Taking Part in Dementia and Neuroprogressive Research

A mock participant-facing information page designed to practise clear, accessible research communication for people living with dementia, neurological conditions and their carers.

Why take part in research?

Taking part in research can help improve understanding, diagnosis, treatment and care for people living with dementia and other neuroprogressive conditions.

What might happen at a research visit?

A research visit may include:

  • Speaking with a member of the research team
  • Reading or discussing participant information
  • Giving informed consent if you choose to take part
  • Answering questions about memory, thinking, mood or daily life
  • Completing cognitive assessments
  • Providing health information relevant to the study
  • Having time for breaks if needed

Cognitive assessments

Some studies include cognitive assessments. These may involve questions or tasks looking at memory, attention, language, problem-solving or visuospatial skills.

These assessments are not intended to embarrass or judge the participant. They are used to help researchers understand patterns of thinking and memory.

Consent and choice

Taking part in research is voluntary. A person can ask questions, take time to decide, decline to take part, or withdraw from a study in line with the study process.

Carers and study partners

Some dementia studies involve a carer, family member or study partner. They may help provide information, support attendance, or discuss changes in memory, mood or daily functioning.

Accessibility

Research information should be clear, respectful and accessible. Participants may need extra time, breaks, large print information, support with hearing or vision needs, or explanations repeated calmly.

Governance note
